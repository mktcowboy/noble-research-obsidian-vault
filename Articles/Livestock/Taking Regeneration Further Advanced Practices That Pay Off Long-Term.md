# Taking Regeneration Further: Advanced Practices That Pay Off Long-Term

> Browse: [[Maps/Topics/Livestock Articles|Topic: Livestock]] | [[Maps/Sections/Livestock - Ranch Operations|Section: Ranch Operations]]
> Original: [Noble.org](https://www.noble.org/regenerative-agriculture/taking-regeneration-further-advanced-practices-that-pay-off-long-term/)
> Concepts: [[Concepts/Regenerative Agriculture|Regenerative Agriculture]], [[Concepts/Cattle and Livestock|Cattle and Livestock]], [[Concepts/Ranch Management|Ranch Management]]

Many experienced ranchers will say, in hindsight, it took their land five years to fully realize the benefits of regenerative management. That’s not to say there weren’t noticeable improvements earlier than that, though there does seem to be something about the five-year mark that holds true for ranchers across the country.

There are options for ranchers approaching or just beyond the five-year threshold who want to go even further to improve long-term outcomes for soil, forage, and herd health. We’ve experimented with a few ideas on Noble’s ranches that can help regenerative ranchers build on the six soil health principles to improve community dynamics and ecological health across their ranches.

## Multi-Species Grazing

Multispecies grazing – adding sheep or goats to your cattle ranch – offers soil health and financial advantages. When multiple livestock species live on a single ranch, their unique grazing behaviors and preferences allow for increased plant and soil microbial biodiversity.

“Small ruminants take advantage of other resources in the landscape that cows can’t. Cows eat grass, but what about forbs and brush? That’s where sheep and goats come in,” explains Noble regenerative ranching advisor Josh Gaskamp. Multispecies grazing enhances forage use and stimulates underground ecosystems, boosting microbial activity, organic matter and drought resilience.

Thanks to faster turnover, multiple offspring and higher market value per pound compared to cattle, Gaskamp says small ruminants are more profitable on a per acre and per pound basis, too.

“You can spread out risk with multispecies grazing. When cattle markets are down, typically the sheep and goat market is up, and vice versa,” Gaskamp says. By diversifying revenue streams and reducing dependence on a single market, ranchers can stabilize income and improve profitability over time.

## Combine Trees and Grazing

Silvopasture is the intentional integration of trees, domesticated animals and forages managed together as a single, multilayered production system. In a silvopasture system, ranchers manage the animal, pasture and tree components equally. Silvopasture can start with just a few trees scattered across grazing pastures. “Whether you are planting trees or thinning out native timber, ensuring that adequate sunlight is reaching the forage is important,” says senior regenerative ranching advisor Charles Rohla.

For livestock grazers considering adding a tree-based enterprise, the biggest barrier may be the start-up time and capital needed to cultivate the trees and nurture then through the pre-income growth years. “It’s not easy, and it’s not a deal where you just plant a tree, walk away and hope to come back in seven or eight years for the harvest,” Rohla says. “It takes time, hard work and you have to be intentional in your management of the trees, the animals and your soil.”

Silvopasture can be accomplished with any tree species. Some species add secondary revenue (nut, fruit, timber, clipping), while other tree species could be used to attract beneficial insects and pollinators. Certain species could be used to increase fertility in the soil (leguminous trees – locust, coffeetree, redbuds, etc.), by adding nitrogen to the soil to help fertilize the forage.

Typically, trees will have a higher relationship with beneficial fungi that can assist forage in nutrient uptake. Trees help cover the soil surface, slow erosion and help lower soil temperature and evaporation as well as forage temperature and transpiration. There are also benefits to livestock in this combined system. “The nutrient concentration and palatability of the forage in a silvopasture system is typically higher than full sun exposure. The animals tend to be less stressed and perform better,” says Rohla.

## Stockpiling Forage

Year-round grazing via a forage bank, or stockpiling pasture forage for winter, can help ranchers cut costs and improve long-term sustainability. One of Noble’s senior regenerative ranching advisors, Jim Johnson, works with ranchers on this topic often; he feels most of them are falling short due to overstocking.

“Getting your stocking right at or below carrying capacity is probably going to get you 90% of the way [to year-round grazing],” says Johnson. He encourages producers to answer a few questions when planning their forage bank: “When do I want to use it? So, when do I need to let it recover? So, when can I graze it prior to that?”

At Noble’s Red River Ranch in southern Oklahoma, stockpiling forage is just one part of the year-round grazing equation. Ranch manager Kevin Pierce also plants cool- and warm-season cover crops to use as standing forage in the winter and early spring. Pierce plants a warm-season mix around June 1, grazes the field in early August, then leaves it to rest until it’s needed as standing forage in December.

Johnson is quick to point out the soil health principle at play here – living roots in the soil through every season. But there’s another reason to consider cover crops. “The economics would be a lot better, for one, because you’re not paying to cut, rake, bale, and haul it off, then haul it back and feed it to (the cattle).”

Every rancher’s regenerative journey is unique. That’s why “context” is one of the core principles – no two ranches are exactly the same. But every rancher can learn and evolve their management in response to the land.

Being curious and open-minded got you this far. Now is the time to continue what’s working well for your land and your business, while asking “what’s next?” Better is possible for your land and your business.
